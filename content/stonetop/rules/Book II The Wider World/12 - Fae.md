# Fae

Strange folk live in the **[[23 - The Great Wood#The Great Wood|Great Wood]]** and **[[13 - Ferrier's Fen#Ferrier's Fen|Ferrier's Fen]]**. Wondrous and dangerous, beings out of legend. The **[[17 - Forest Folk#Forest Folk|Forest Folk]]** called them "Good Neighbors" and "Hidden Ones," and took pains to avoid insulting them. They cautioned Stonetop to do the same. And while the Forest Folk are gone, the Fae remain.

The **[[24 - Green Lords#Names|Green Lords]]** bred the Fae long ago, to herd spirits and to weave wondrous realms. They relied on the Fae to manage their estates, to build the **[[53 - Waystones#Waystones|waystones]]**, and, later, to create the Green Lord's idyllic afterlives.

Some Fae resented their servitude. Some found loopholes in the promises and oaths that bound them. Ringleaders arose, creating webs of debt and obligation that undermined their masters' hold.

The Green Lords grew desperate. They called on the **[[46 - The Things Below#The Things Below|Things Below]]**, corrupted themselves with unholy power. They bred living weapons, hunted down the rebels, and took terrible vengeance.

The uprising nearly failed, but the Green Lords had drawn the ire of their fellow Makers. The **[[43 - Stone Lords#Stone Lords|Stone Lords]]**, in particular, were horrified by the Green Lords' corruption. They worked a terrible spell to shatter the heart of the Green Lord's domain, turning it into the ruined bog that today we call Ferrier's Fen. Then they allied with the Fae, and waged war against the Green Lords who survived.

After the siege of **[[52 - Vor Svetelik#Vor Svetelik|Vor Svetelik]]**, the Fae found themselves adrift and bereft of purpose. Many were no longer bound by oath or obligation. Ringleaders bickered and squabbled. Factions formed. Armies disbanded. And the Fae became the fractured people they are today.

## **The good neighbors**

Fae and mortals cross paths, of course. The Forest Folk descended from the Green Lord's mortal servants, and dwelt in the Great Wood for centuries. Fae fought side-by-side with the Stone Lords' human slaves. And some Fae, certainly, know of the humans living on their borders: Stonetop, **[[32 - Marshedge#Marshedge|Marshedge]]**, the **[[34 - North Manmarch#North Manmarch|Manmarchers]]**, even some **[[54 - The Whitefang Mountains#1d6 point of interest|Whitefang villages]]**. There have always been dealings between them and us.

But Fae are not like us. They breathe magic. Time means little to them. They have no base needs. They are driven by pride, boredom, and obsession. They are shaped by their debts, their obligations, and the ancient scars of servitude and war.

Fae are *dangerous* to mortals. Maybe not hostile (though some are), but alien. Some admire our spark of life, our creativity and passion. Some find our memories and our emotions rich and heady. Some delight in our dignity or our short-lived antics. Some might even care about us. But they also think no more of stealing an infant from the crib than we would at picking a pup out of a litter.

## **Names**

Fae "names" tend to be titles, nouns, or descriptive phrases or borrowed from those they've met—simple, whimsical, ominous, or grandiose as befits their nature.

Mix, match, and riff on words related to plants and beasts and rocks, features of the landscape, natural phenomena, colors, mundane objects, emotions or emotional activities, titles, relations, and local names.

Some simple names: Acorn, Cobweb, Hob, Jackdaw, Kettle, Lark, Moth, Quills, Rill, Robin, Stewpot, Trillium, Uncle Whiskers, Whistler, Wobbles, Yellowtail

Some whimsical names: Bellweather, Bitterlips, Moonbeam, Peasebottom, Poppy o' Twilight, Rainpetal, Stagthorn, Thistledust, Whiteoak, Yellow Britches

Some ominous names: Bonekettle, Father Longfingers, Grinning Nel, the Hangman, the King of Dirges, Old Sorrows, the Pale Sisters, Stonetooth, the Weeping Rose

Some grandiose names: Dawnwhisper, Fireblossom, Breath of Thunder, Hare Starblade, the Midnight Mare, Rain Upon the Valley, Silverstag, the Storm King

Some Fae might go by many names, or change their names over time. If a given Fae is known to the locals, consider what they call it in their local tongue. A Fae named Moonbeam, for example, might be called "Pelydrlloer" in Stonetop or "Mondstrohl" by the Manmarchers.

Perhaps each Fae has a true and secret name, by which they can be commanded and bound. If so, it must be their most treasured and precious secret.

# **Fae nature**

The Fae are living beings made of spiritstuff. They aren't quite spirits, but interact with spirits as if they were. They're made of flesh and bone, or close enough, but it is strange, malleable, and resilient. They…

- … need not eat or drink, but can enjoy such fare;
- … need not sleep, but often choose to; … can suffocate or drown, but usually
- recover when they can breathe again; … feel no drive to reproduce, but might enjoy sex, fall in love, sire or bear children, steal them from mortals;
- … do not grow feeble with age, but can grow bored, deeply set in their ways;
- … cannot break a promise or an oath, but are keen at finding loopholes; and
- … feel the weight of debts and insults as though they were heavy chains.

# **Injury**

Fae are unnaturally tough (Armor 4) and can heal any wound not caused by iron. A Fae who suffers a "mortal" wound might pass through the Last Door. But they might also change their mind and come back later, for Fae are fickle things.

#### **Iron**

Iron (including steel) is offensive to Fae. Its presence insults them. It disrupts their magic. It burns their skin, ignores their Armor, leaves scars that never fade. Iron can kill a Fae, truly kill them dead.

**[[36 - Primordial Powers#**Primordial powers**|Black iron]]** is even worse. Its presence unravels their magic. Its touch doesn't just burn, it suppurates their flesh (+1d6 damage, ignores armor, messy). It leaves vicious, gaping wounds that never heal and that eventually turn them into **[[12 - Fae#**The Bloody Boots**|Hollow Fae]]**.

### **Magic and the Fae**

Fae are Fae, neither spirit nor mortal. As a rule, magic that specifically affects "spirits" or "mortals" does not apply to the Fae. They are their own thing.

With that said, magic is fuzzy and each Fae is unique. When in doubt, consider what feels right in this particular case. A ploy that would work in a fairy tale should probably work against the Fae!

## **Illusions and glamours**

Illusions are lies. They have no substance. An illusionary chair bears no weight; the illusion of a sword does not cut. A skillful Fae might create the illusion of pain and blood when you are cut with an illusionary sword, but the wound has no lasting effect.

Glamour is more profound. It changes a thing and how it interacts with the world, at least until the glamour fades. Glamour a rock into a chair and sit comfortably. Glamour a stick into a sword and it will cut, make you bleed real blood. When the glamour ends, the wound remains.

Glamours are like poetry. One might glamour an acorn into a gem, or fluttering leaves into butterflies, or a braying mortal's head into that of a donkey. But one does not just, say, glamour a spear into a puddle of water. These things must rhyme!

Fae can see through each other's illusions and glamours, but as a rule they play along. Doing otherwise can be insulting.

Glamour affects neither iron nor steel, and the touch of either causes illusions to flicker and fade. The touch of **[[36 - Primordial Powers#**Primordial powers**|black iron]]** dispels both illusions and glamours, like soap dropped in oily water.

## **Language**

Fae understand anyone they meet, and can always make themselves understood. Many speak in an affected manner (accented, in verse, with odd sentence construction, etc.) to amuse themselves, to annoy others, or because that's just how they think.

## **Debts and promises**

Fae are bound by debts, obligations, and promises. This applies to mortals who treat with them, too. A Fae might:

- <sup>ä</sup> Make an offer (fair or lopsided)
- <sup>ä</sup> Politely decline or dissemble
- <sup>ä</sup> Insist, ask for more, offer less
- <sup>ä</sup> Call in a debt, use it as leverage
- <sup>ä</sup> Make or demand a promise
- <sup>ä</sup> Find/exploit a loophole
- <sup>ä</sup> Trade a debt to another Fae

When a *Fae is beholden to you*, you have advantage on any roll to resist or defend yourself against it. When you *leverage a debt that they owe you or a promise they've made*, gain advantage to Persuade or otherwise influence it.

When you *are beholden to a Fae*, you have disadvantage on any roll made directly against them (including damage rolls). When you *fail to uphold a promise you've made to a Fae*, for any reason, either you are beholden to that Fae or they take it as an insult (their choice).

When you *pass through the Last Door*, all debts you owe and promises you have made are null and void.

## **Insults and etiquette**

Fae are also bound by insults, pride, and etiquette. An insulted Fae feels the need for satisfaction like a mortal feels hunger, as a gnawing and physical thing. Their culture is full of little rules and points of etiquette that mortals often stumble over.

One might insult a Fae by…

- … rudely refusing an offer;
- … failing to honor a debt or promise;
- … taking what they think they own;
- … making demands or threats without the proper leverage;
- … sneaking iron into their presence;
- … failing to play along with their games;
- … disparaging their honor/their power/ their cunning/whatever they value about themselves; or
- … accusing them of any of the above.

When the PCs deal with a Fae, that Fae might:

- <sup>ä</sup> Set the PCs up for a faux pas
- <sup>ä</sup> Make an accusation
- <sup>ä</sup> Act affronted, genuinely or as a ploy
- <sup>ä</sup> Truly take insult

And if insulted, they might:

- <sup>ä</sup> Demand satisfaction
- <sup>ä</sup> Storm/scamper off
- <sup>ä</sup> Offer their forgiveness, as a gift
- <sup>ä</sup> Ignore all other rules of etiquette
- <sup>ä</sup> Spread tales or rumors
- <sup>ä</sup> Hold a grudge, lay plans for revenge
- <sup>ä</sup> Resort to violence or magic

## Fae domains

Fae domains are hidden realities, existing between the physical and spirit worlds, touching and overlapping both but part of neither. Some are tiny, intimate, cozy. Others are vast and grand.

Not every place where a Fae dwells is truly a domain. Plenty of Fae squat in ruins or caves or hollow trees, perhaps spruced up with glamour but fully in this world. Domains are different, set apart.

To create a Fae domain, start with the real-world terrain it borders or overlaps with. Most are found in the Great Wood [[23 - The Great Wood#The Great Wood|The Great Wood]], in the Foothills [[16 - The Foothills|The Foothills]], or in Ferrier's Fen [[13 - Ferrier's Fen#Ferrier's Fen|Ferrier’s Fen]]; those in the Fen are often corrupted [[46 - The Things Below#**Corrupted being**|The Things Below]]. Many are part of Green Lord ruins [[24 - Green Lords#Ruins|Ruins]].

Once the real-world terrain is established, pick or roll for its entrance . Interpret the result in light of where it's found.

## 1d6 entrance

- 1 A shimmer, a feeling, little more
- 2 Megalith(s)/a waystone [[53 - Waystones|Waystones]]
- 3 A door/gate/opening, natural or made, literal or just an impression
- 4 A ring of toadstools or some other distinctive pattern of flora (perhaps [[50 - Useful or Valuable Flora#**Useful or valuable flora**|useful or valuable]])
- 5 A path/a climb/a descent/a fall
- 6 Only active at certain times (in moonlight, at sunset, in winter, etc.) and roll again

If an entrance in the Great Wood is at least 10 years old, it's likely to be marked by Forest Folk glyphs [[17 - Forest Folk#**Hooks**|Forest Folk]].

For the domain itself, pick or roll for a theme [[12 - Fae#1d6 entrance|Fae]], the domain's original purpose , the anchor that maintains it, and 1-2 elements . Maybe roll 1d6 to inform its size and scale.

## 1d6 purpose

- 1 Containment: to imprison/preserve
- 2 Habitat: where Fae live/work/play
- 3 Community: a place to gather, trade, gossip, show off, travel
- 4 Status: a monument, a place to impose, someone showing off
- 5 Creativity: an expression, an exploration, a work of art
- 6 Pleasure: a paradise, a retreat, a false afterlife, a playground

- 2 The instinct or intent of multiple Fae, dwelling (trapped?) within
- 3 A shard of power left by a powerful Fae, buried or hidden away
- 4 One or more Fae, bound/inert, their essence sunk into the domain
- 5 Rune-carved stone infused with Fae power, a work of the Green Lords
- 6 Roll again, but it's failing, fickle, unstable, possibly abandoned

**1d6** element

- 1 Exit(s) to **[[53 - Waystones#Waystones|Fae paths]]** or far-off place(s) in the world
- 2 Surreal landscapes: a forest cave, an underwater kingdom, a castle in the clouds, a tiny place made large, a mundane place made giant, etc.
- 3 "Civilized" things, out of place: a feast hall in a mire, a house carved from a tree, a library in a cave, etc.
- 4 A real place, plucked from the world and the passing of time
- 5 A vast and shifting place, easy to get lost in, always something new
- 6 Decay/squalor/disorder, perhaps hidden by glamour or illusion

Populate the domain with Fae and other inhabitants that make sense. Also fill it with **[[12 - Fae#Artifacts|artifacts]]**, and any domain **[[12 - Fae#Dangers|hazards]]** that make sense. **[[12 - Fae#**Fluid time**|Time dilation]]** is a particular concern to mortal visitors!

### **Entering domains**

Some entrances work only at particular times: under a full moon, during winter, at sunrise or sunset, etc. This holds true for both Fae and mortal alike.

Otherwise, Fae can freely use the entrance of most domains, like you or I might open a door. For mortals, it's more involved.

**Something interesting:** A mortal might blunder into a Fae domain by accident, or follow a guide, or be drawn into one by a mischievous or malevolent Fae. Or they might fall asleep near an entrance and visit the domain in their dreams. Iron or steel might protect you, or not; you might just find that all your iron was left behind in the world.

**Something useful:** To enter a Fae domain on purpose, you must leave behind all iron and steel, plus one of the following:

- Follow the footsteps of a Fae guide
- Addle (open?) your mind with drugs/ alcohol/madness/intense emotion/etc.
- Have Fae blood in your veins
- Be able to see through Fae magic and glamours, via **[[57 - Appendix C - Minor Arcana#A small, strange book fragile|Old Mother's Wine]]** or a similar effect

#### **Locked entrances**

Some entrances have special requirements to open them, for both Fae and mortal alike. These requirements might involve…

- … singing/reciting/inscribing the proper words;
- … doing something dangerous, without flinching (like running at the wall);
- … doing something daft (like spinning 'til you fall);
- … paying the toll (a secret, a story, a thing of beauty, a promise, etc.);
- … convincing the guardians to let you in;
- … some variation or combination of the above; or
- … something else entirely.

The residents of a domain might ignore the requirements of a locked entrance; the creator of a domain almost certainly can.

## **Fluid time**

Time passes strangely in Fae domains, when it passes at all. Upon returning to the world, one might find that no time has passed, or that time has passed normally, or that they've lost days, weeks, months, or years. Tales tell of folk returning to the world centuries later and crumbling into dust, but those are just tales. Right?

Lost time most often afflicts those who:

- Eat Fae food or drink Fae drink
- Fall asleep
- Lose themselves to revels, work, etc.
- Insult/become indebted to the locals
- Disrupt a place held in stasis

Threaten PCs with lost time, for sure. Use it to explain the absence of PCs whose players have to miss some sessions. But don't have the PCs lose years or decades unless you—and your players—really want to shake up your game.

## **Some Fae domains**

- A little door in a rocky outcrop, leading to a dying crystal city of tiny Fae and their talking vole subjects.
- A natural arch of hawthorne trees. Beyond: a tree-lined hall, a runecarved hearth, endless drink & dance.
- A knot in a large dead tree. One must recite a certain poem to enter. Inside is a crowded theater, where callous Fae call up long-dead shades to play out their mortal dramas.
- A **[[53 - Waystones#Waystones|waystone]]** in the mire. A crossroad of many Fae paths, oftused but one must pay a toll of beauty to the wretched troll who guards it.
- A wide stream. Fall face-first into it and enter a watery, dreamlike realm. A catfish-like Fae arranges stones, treasures, and lost memories into a beautiful, ever-shifting mosaic.
- A circle of menhirs, entwined in thorns. Can only be entered in moonlight. Inside, all the stones but one are revealed to be **[[17 - Forest Folk#Forest Folk|Forest Folk]]**, bound in agony.

## **The Wandering Market**

Little ties the Fae together, except for a desire to show off. And for that, they need an audience. A place to meet and gather. A place to trade stories and wonders and lies. They need the Wandering Market.

#### **Questions**

- According to tales, when does the Wandering Market convene?
- If you could purchase any treasure from the Fae, one wondrous thing, what would it be?
- What is your single most treasured possession? Why do you value it so?
- Who among you is most beautiful? Has the best singing voice? Dances the finest? Makes the best food?

#### **Getting there**

The Wandering Market is a **[[12 - Fae#**Getting there**|Fae domain]]**, made anew by a different host each time it convenes.

To find the market, one must know when it is happening and find an entrance. The entrances are many, but different each time. Any Fae knows where the nearest entrance lies—pick or roll for the terrain near which it is found [[23 - The Great Wood#. steading improvement .|The Great Wood]] for the **[[13 - Ferrier's Fen#**Questions**|Great Wood]]**, for **Ferrier's Fen**), and pick some whimsical requirement such as:

- Climb the seventh alder tree you find; it's up there
- Blindfold yourself; follow the bird who calls your name
- Lay a path of stones into the hollow of a tree; walk the path at sundown
- Go to sleep in a patch of bluebells, and visit the market in your dreams

- Sensory overload: crowds and barkers, silks and spices, perfumes, feathers and flowers, leering inhuman faces

- Snide comments about how this Market pales to previous ones
- Squeals of delight at your smile, your wry comment, your whistled tune
- How long have you been here, again?

## **Moves**

## **TREASURE HUNT**

When you *scour the Wandering Market for a solution*, say what problem you're hoping to solve and roll +CHA: **on a 7+**, you find someone offering just the thing, and can Haggle for it. But before you do, **on a 10+**, you must first pick 1; **on a 7-9**, you must first pick 3:

- Someone else is trying to buy it (or pretending to); you must Haggle with disadvantage
- It's not actually a solution itself, but rather a way to acquire one
- It's cursed, stolen, or otherwise dangerous to use or possess
- To even find it, you've put yourself in some Fae's debt

## **HAGGLE**

When you *dicker with a Fae* (anywhere, not just in the Wandering Market), roll +WIS. Roll with advantage if they like you, and disadvantage if they wish you ill. **On a 12+**, it costs but a pittance; **on a 10-11**, pick 1; **on a 7-9**, pick 2; **on a 6-**, pick 3:

- The price is Value 2, at least
- The price is irreplaceable, cherished, heartbreaking to lose
- The price is not truly yours to pay
- It involves a promise, condition, or obligation, sure to cause trouble
- You end up beholden to them

Whatever the price, you've either agreed to it or you've insulted the Fae and the deal is off. Work together to figure out exactly what happened. Reneging on the deal is certain to insult the Fae and/or put you in their **[[12 - Fae#Debts and promises|debt]]**.

## . trade opportunities .

If the PCs get a trade opportunity (from the Seasons Change move), the Fae might offer the following. The PCs still need to Haggle for it, though!

- An impossible task, like a whole season's worth of work done in a night
- A secret, a memory, an answer to a mystery, help with a sticky problem, etc.
- Fantastical trade goods (Value 2 or 3): cloth-of-clouds, mead brewed from dreams, dyes of glamour, etc.
- A **[[12 - Fae#Artifacts|Fae artifact]]**
- An artifact of the **[[24 - Green Lords#**Artifacts**|Green Lords]]**, the **[[17 - Forest Folk#**Artifacts**|Forest Folk]]**, the **[[41 - Spirits of the Wild#Artifacts|spirits of the wild]]**, or perhaps the **[[46 - The Things Below#**Unnatural phenomena**|Things Below]]**.

## **The Red Pennant**

, *magical*, Blood lll

A woolen flag hanging from a crossbar at the end of a sturdy long spear. It's tattered, crimson, with a crudely embroidered boot for a device. It hangs heavy. Like it's wet.

When you *fly the Red Pennant in battle*, all who fight on your side feel bloodlust boil within them. Hold 3 Blood. While the battle rages, you can spend Blood 1-for-1 to will your allies to:

- Rush forward, driving back their foes
- Hold fast, making their foes pay for every inch
- Fight savagely, giving no quarter

If spending Blood results in you Ordering Followers or Deploying, or another PC Clashing, the roll has advantage. Each time you spend Blood, those affected gain some small, animalistic or stony trait (little horns, weird eyes, crag-like wrinkles, etc.).

When you *spend the last Blood or the battle ends*, the Red Pennant's color fades and a few (~1 in 6) of those manifested traits will linger, especially in those who fought hardest. It will not function again until you soak it in the blood of fallen foes.

This is the battle standard of the **[[12 - Fae#**Blush-of-Dawn**|Bloody Boots]]**, their most sacred relic. Don't expect them to just let it go.

## **The Bear of Winter**

*Solitary*, *huge*, *fae*, *magical*, *hoarder*, *devious*, *fierce*, *terrifying*

**HP** 26; **Armor** 5 (resilience, ice), 2 vs. iron (hide, ice)

**Damage** bite or maul d12+5 (*close*, *hand*, *reach*, *forceful*, *grabby*, *messy*, 1 piercing)

**[[12 - Fae#Magic and the Fae|Special qualities Fae nature]]**; immune to cold; aura of wintry weather, matching his mood

**Instinct** to protect his status and holdings

- <sup>ä</sup> Weave ice and snow, glamours and illusions, entire Fae domains
- <sup>ä</sup> Send a minion (spirit, beast, or indebted Fae) to do his bidding
- <sup>ä</sup> Wield the wintry elements
- <sup>ä</sup> Take the form of a scarred old man or a storm of sleet and snow
- <sup>ä</sup> Make use of a forgotten artifact

When winter snows blanket the Great Wood, the Bear roams the land. He's most often met as a great and snuffling beast, big as a house, icicles clinging to his fur. His retinue follows: nervously, discretely, waiting upon his call. He visits the many Fae who owe him favors and those who have pledged him their support. None are happy to see him. None dare turn him away.

The Bear, you see, is among the most powerful of the Fae. Snapped his own master's neck. Gobbled down its corpse. He freed hundreds of Fae from servitude. He leveraged the debts they owed him to become something of a feudal lord.

The Bear's domain is a great cave, full of hoarded riches and slinking servants. It has many entrances and exits, each leading to a different cave mouth in the world, but only when that cave mouth is surrounded by drifts of snow.

## **The Four Chieftains**

The chieftains are among the most powerful of the Fae, personally and politically. The Bear of Winter is one of them. The others might include:

- **The Warbler**, who dwells in the springtime canopy and fills the world with song. An incorrigible gossip, who pretends to be a fool. Often goes incognito. Extremely well-informed.
- **The Crowned Stag**, who troops through deep summer woods with their court and hangers-on. Beautiful, proud, dashing, and noble. Lusty, too, and prone to fall in love.
- **Longshadow**, a stately old tree-wife, bare of leaves. Found brooding in the autumn forest, attended by skittish minions. Speaks prophecy, lays subtle plans. Knows that the war isn't over.

## **Hollow Fae**

 *Solitary*, *fae*, *undead*, *confused*, *terrifying*, see below

**HP** 19; **Armor** 5 (resilience, lacks vitals), 1 vs. black iron

**Damage** life-drain d10 (*hand*, *close*, *area*, ignores armor)

**Special qualities** senses life; surrounded by inert death and ash; can be truly destroyed only by black iron

**Instinct** to consume life-force

- <sup>ä</sup> Moan, filling all with dread
- <sup>ä</sup> Tear apart Fae magic
- <sup>ä</sup> Pause in confusion when confronted with who it once was
- <sup>ä</sup> Open wide, drink in the vitality of the living (inflicting debilities)

A Fae wounded by **[[36 - Primordial Powers#**Primordial powers**|black iron]]** will pray for death. For to survive such a wound is a curse. They grow weak, then confused, then hungry. Soon, they become a walking husk, surrounded by death, inhaling any source of life they find.

Hollow Fae are no longer vulnerable to mere iron or steel. Debts and promises no longer bind them. They will tear their way free of any Fae domain. Only black iron can destroy them, and what living Fae would be fool enough to try?

When faced with such a terror, most Fae choose to flee. A few might be so clever as to trap it in some forgotten tomb. But the cleverest, oh, the cleverest… they find some fool mortal to handle the problem for them.

A *large* Hollow Fae is tougher than shown above: 23 HP and +1 damage, +*reach*. A *huge* one is tougher still, with 27 HP, +3 damage, and +*reach*.
