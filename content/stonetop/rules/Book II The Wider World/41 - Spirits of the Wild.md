# Spirits of the Wild

The physical world—this mortal realm of base matter and flesh—is but one reality. There is another, hidden reality. Arguably a truer reality. Here, spirits dwell.

The spirit realm is filled with entities, especially those of the natural world. Each beast, each tree, each gust of wind or flickering flame—they all have spirits. So do the soil, the rocks, the mountains and the streams.

Most of these spirits are quiet, slumbering or barely conscious or operating on a scale so alien as to be impenetrable to human minds. But some mortals can speak to them nonetheless, and goad them into action. Sometimes, these sleepers shift and mumble and stretch of their own accord.

The most active spirits are those tethered to physical objects, or those associated with beasts or other "animate" things—things full of life and energy. These are the spirits that mortals encounter most often.

## **Hooks**

- A villager (or visitor) has been cursed by a powerful elemental spirit, maybe in a way that puts others in danger.
- A child hears voices and sees spirits; maybe they're crippled with fear, or maybe they recklessly ask spirits for petty favors.
- Something that the PCs want can be found in in a sacred site, one that's known to house unfriendly, powerful spirits.
- Someone has unwittingly brought a spirit's tether into the village, and it starts wreaking havoc.
- A PC learns that a particular spirit knows an important secret, something they desperately want to learn.

## **Regarding spirits**

The following apply not just to spirits of the wild, but also to **[[10 - Death and the Undying#Ghosts|ghosts]]**, **[[10 - Death and the Undying#Dool spirit|dool spirits]]**, some **[[36 - Primordial Powers#Primordial powers|primordial powers]]**, the **[[46 - The Things Below#The Things Below|Things Below]]**, and **[[20 - Gods and Religion#Little gods|little gods]]**.

### **Manifestation**

Spirits lack physical form, existing in the realm of thought and symbol. They can perceive and influence the mortal realm in subtle ways, but to directly affect it, a spirit must manifest or possess a physical form.

Each spirit manifests differently. Some shape a body from crude matter. Others appear as intangibles: cold, shadow, moonlight, etc. Some can only "ride" living beings. Others can animate beings that were once alive, or vessels specifically prepared to hold them. A rare few can manifest bodies of pure spirit-stuff.

Spirits manifest or possess forms that make symbolic sense. A fire spirit won't possess water; it might manifest as a flame, or animate a cloud of cinders, or possess a hot-headed youth.

### **Tethers**

Some spirits are bound to a thing in the physical world. The tether might occur naturally (like a brennauwed and its tree), by choice or mutual benefit (like an altar where mortals leave offerings for the spirt), or via trickery, force, or coercion.

A tether anchors and empowers its spirit, granting it a stronger connection to the physical world. If harmed or weakened, the spirit can retreat to its tether to rejuvenate.

But a tether is not just a boon; it's also a vulnerability. Harming the tether harms the spirit. Destroying the tether destroys the spirit—or at least weakens, diminishes, and greatly changes it.

Some tethers don't just anchor their spirits: they bind and imprison them. A spirit so bound can't leave its tether's presence. The most powerful bindings seal their spirits away, greatly restricting their perceptions and ability to influence the world. But no binding is perfect, and a spirit so bound might tempt or trick others into releasing it.

### **Fighting Spirits**

One can fight a spirit only when it manifests, and even then they can be difficult to harm. Manifest forms often have lots of HP and might be vulnerable to only certain materials or attacks.

Even if one can harm a manifestation, they're not really harming the spirit. Reducing it to 0 HP ends the manifestation. This weakens the spirit, forcing it to retreat to its tether (if it has one). But it doesn't truly harm it.

To truly harm a spirit, one must use potent magic, damage or destroy its tether, or fight it directly in the spirit realm. A spirit reduced to 0 HP in one of these ways is actually destroyed—or at least so thoroughly diminished that it won't be a problem for many human lifetimes.

You might want to track a spirit's "manifest" HP vs. "true" HP. If the PCs hurt a manifest spirit with something that could truly harm it, reduce both sets of HP.

## **Communion**

One might wish to commune with a spirit to seek knowledge or request aid. The Blessed in particular is able to sense active spirits, speak with them, and force even dormant spirits to manifest and hear them out. Some NPCs and monsters have similar powers.

Most people, though, are unaware of the spirits around them. Spirits of the wild tend to ignore individual humans. The best way for most mortals to commune with a spirit is to enter a sacred site (carefully, respectfully) and make an offering that gets the spirit's attention. One could also intentionally provoke the spirit—getting it to manifest—but that rarely ends well.

One might also seek out a spirit in its own realm. This requires shifting one's consciousness to the spirit realm, which might involve…

- … meditation;
- … imbibing drugs/alcohol;
- … exhaustion or deprivation;
- … percussion, music, dancing;
- … a guide or teacher; and/or
- … a magic item or spell.

Once a mortal's consciousness is in the spirit realm, they must find the spirit(s) in question, get their attention, and convince it to help. None of these are easy. None of these are safe. If a PC attempts such a journey, Chart a Course with them through the spirit world. Good requirements and challenges include…

- … they need a knowledgeable guide or detailed instructions;
- … they risk getting lost;
- … the way will be grueling, they risk exhaustion; and/or
- … they must watch out for **[[10 - Death and the Undying#Specter|specters]]**, **[[10 - Death and the Undying#Wraith|wraiths]]**, or other **[[10 - Death and the Undying#Ghosts|ghosts]]**; **[[10 - Death and the Undying#Dool spirit|dool spirits]]**; **[[36 - Primordial Powers#Fundamental|fundamentals]]**; **[[36 - Primordial Powers#Voidblight|voidblights]]**; spirits **corrupted** by the Things Below [[46 - The Things Below#The Things Below|The Things Below]]; or just **elemental spirits** that are territorial, or violent, or powerful and careless.

Depending on the spirit that they seek, they might also need to surmount/brave/cross a hazardous stretch of the spirit realm and/or risk drawing the attention of certain spirits. For example, if they sought out the spirit of **[[21 - The Golden Oak#The Golden Oak|the Golden Oak]]**, you might tell them...

- … they risk drawing the attention of the crossing's guardian; and/or
- … they need to brave the secret crossing itself, which borders the Last Door and the realm of the dead.

## **Elemental spirits**

These spirits mostly just do their thing, with little concern for humanity unless some mortal offends them or seeks them out for aid. An elemental spirit can:

- Reveal what it knows, from its own perspective
- Spread word to other spirits
- Bestow a blessing or curse, tied to its element
- Shift, move, or direct that which falls in its domain

Elemental spirits rarely manifest. When they do, they often possess or animate the element itself, or appear as ghostly figures somehow reflecting their element. They're more likely to be NPCs than *monsters*.

## **Sacred sites**

These are places where spirits of the wild are tethered, or where they tend to be active. Pick or roll for terrain based on the region, theme [[41 - Spirits of the Wild#Spirits of the wild|Spirits of the Wild]] to determine the nature of the spirit(s) present, a marker, and the spirits' activity and disposition. Consider rolling the Die of Fate to determine the spirit or spirits' relative power.

### **1d12** marker

- 1 Nothing you can put your finger on, just the sense of being watched
- 2-4 A place that resonates strongly with the spirit's theme
- 5-6 A remarkable natural feature, particularly large/beautiful/ancient/etc.
- 7-8 An unusual feature, probably natural but strange or out of place
- 9 An idol or altar, ancient and crumbling/buried/submerged
- 10 An idol or altar, made by the locals
- 11-12 Roll twice with a d10, combine

### **1d12** activity

- 1 Missing, dissipated, greatly weakened
- 2 Imprisoned, probably seeking release
- 3-4 Slumbering, waiting for something
- 5-6 Sporadic, often slumbering or away
- 7-9 Alert, aware, unlikely to manifest
- 10-12 Alert, aware, manifesting freely

### **1d12** disposition

- 1-2 Dangerous, possibly corrupted by **[[46 - The Things Below#The Things Below|the Things Below]]**
- 3-4 Vengeful, grumpy, protective, angry at intrusion
- 5-6 Plaintive, needful, wronged, demanding
- 7-8 Aloof, shy, dismissive, has their own concerns
- 9-10 Curious, playful, mischievous, friendly
- 11-12 Content, pleasant, helpful, beneficent

*The PCs are in the Steplands, heading towards Three Coven Lake, and they've just run afoul of some Hillfolk. They escaped, but only because the Hillfolk turned back from the cave they hid in, like they were scared to enter. I think this is a sacred site. I roll a 3 for theme (air/wind/weather/fire) and a 4 for the marker (place that resonates with the theme). I'm picturing one of those eternal flames, where a methane pocket seeps out and burns forever. I get 10 for activity (alert, aware, manifesting freely) and roll a 12 for disposition (content, pleasant, helpful, beneficent) which is odd given the Hillfolk's reaction.*

*So: a cave lit by a flame with no apparent source, issuing from a crack in the earth. The flame houses an old, powerful, talkative spirit that's been burning since the days of the Makers. It blames the Hillfolk for their ancestors' role in calling up the Things Below and toppling the Stone Lords, so it's hostile towards them. It'll be right helpful to the Blessed and her friends!*

## **Artifacts**

### **Length of braided leather**

*magical*

3 feet long, frayed on one end, and crusty with flaking... something. The braids entwine 2d4 beads of redwood. Each bead contains some harm: a wound (1d10 damage, *messy*, ignores armor), a sickness, a poison, a delusion. The whole thing binds a spirit of blight.

When you *unweave the braid and let a redwood bead drop upon the earth*, the spirit manifests as a warm, damp wind. Name someone in your presence; the spirit inflicts upon them whatever harm was stored in the bead. If you do not name someone in your presence, the spirit will choose someone at random (maybe even you).

### **A tightly-woven net**

*close*, *thrown*, *magical*, *crude*

Woven from Flats-grass by a Hillfolk sorcerer or witch. Hundreds of minor root-spirits are tethered to the strands.

When you *cast the net over a spirit*, it is ensnared—even if its form is intangible or could flow through the net. The spirit can't escape on its own as long as the net is intact. Don't try to use this on fire spirits—they'll burn right through it.

### **A fur-lined bedroll**

◇, *magical*

This bedroll is decorated with Forest Folk beadwork and lined with the softest rabbit fur. It tethers a nurturing, protective spirit, one that is quiet and comforting.

When you *Make Camp in the fur-lined bedroll*, you regain 1d6 (extra) HP and gain advantage on your next roll.

### **A stone cup**

*magical*

A lump of limestone, big as a large man's fist, sheared off smooth at one end and hollowed out. Water slowly seeps from the interior wall, filling the cup in about half an hour. The water is hard, mineral-rich but potable, and just a little cursed.

When you *drink water produced by the cup*, you'll have troubling dreams the next time you sleep—dreams of suffocation and endless wet caverns and huge uncaring things stirring in the lightless depths. Roll 1d6: **on a 1**, you wake up screaming and will get no rest this evening; **on a 2-6**, the dreams are merely unsettling.

### **A cracked femur**

◇, *magical*

This leg bone is always cold and covered in rime. It's the bone of a witch who broke her leg and froze to death. It houses not a ghost, but a potent spirit of winter and cold.

When you *first grip the bone*, roll +CON: **on a 10+**, the spirit acknowledges you as master and you can wield the bone; **on a 7-9**, pick 1:

- Drop the bone; you can never wield it
- Endure as the spirit sucks away your heat—take 2d4 damage (ignores armor) and mark *weakened*—but you then master the spirit and can wield the bone.

**On a 6-**, the spirit sucks away your heat—take 2d4 damage (ignores armor) and mark *weakened*—and you can never wield the bone.

When you *wield the bone as a weapon* (*hand*, *crude*, *magical*, ignores armor) it deals 2d4 damage instead of your normal damage, sucking the warmth from your foe.

### **A crown of flowers**

*fragile*, *magical*

A wreath of living vines, sprouting flowers of great variety and beauty. A woman who dons the crown becomes greatly attuned to the fertility of everything around her, and the whispering spirits of growing things. She also becomes aware of the rites of spring.

When a *woman wears the crown and performs the rites of spring*—dancing around an oak tree, making offerings to the spirits, and joining with her lover in the light of the full moon—then her steading rolls with advantage to generate Surplus in both summer and autumn. Also, if the woman wearing the crown so chooses, she will conceive and bear a healthy child, regardless of any barriers.

### **A forked stick of redwood**

◇, *magical*

A stout stick about 2 feet long, the bark removed, with fine woodworm trails making entirely-too-regular patterns. If you hold it gently, with a hand on each tine of the fork, you can feel it tugging, pulling you gently in certain directions.

When you *dowse with the redwood stick*, it will guide you to the nearest site sacred to the spirits of the wild. With practice, you can estimate the distance and even the disposition of the spirits that dwell there.

### **A hunk of amber**

◇, *magical*, Value 2

It's about the size of two fists, with a mass of blood-sucking insects floating in its center. It tethers an ambitious spirit of insects, blight, and disease. Treat it as a threat (type: *magical entity*, **Instinct** to foster worship) with the following moves:

- Demand a blood sacrifice, poured over the amber
- Shield someone from sickness, vermin, and poison
- Blight an enemy with sickness, vermin, or both

## **The Brennauwed**

The Tall Ladies, Daughters-of-Seed, the Slow Sisters: these are all titles given to the Brennauwed, powerful tree spirits and, if you believe them, kin to **[[09 - Danu, the Earth Mother#Danu, the Earth Mother|Danu]]** herself. Each is tethered to a single tree. The younger ones can roam, but the older a Brennauwed is, the more bound she is to her tree.

>  *Solitary, huge, spirit, magical, mesmerizing*
>
>
> **HP** 27; **Armor** 2 (reflexes, swarm)
>
> **Damage** whirlwind of pecks, claws, buffets d8+3 (*hand*, *area*, disadvantage)
>
> **Instinct** to absorb individual identity
>
> - Manifest in a murmuration of starlings (nigh impossible to harm with personal weapons)
> - Strip a swath of land clean of seed, saplings, insects
> - Overwhelm with sound and the weight of numbers
>
> When the sun sets, starlings flock in absurd numbers: thousands upon thousands dancing and wheeling in the sky, like a chittering thunderhead, like a cloud of living smoke.
>
> Farmers do what they can to keep starlings far from town, and not just because they eat precious seeds and bury the land in droppings. They do so for fear of the grwgnach.
>
> The grwgnach is a spirit of the mob, of the many coming together to become something more. It's not a king or a queen of the starlings. Rather, it's what a million starlings become when each bird loses itself to the flock.
>
> When you *gaze rapt upon a murmuration of starlings*, tell us of a time when you felt small and insignificant, or when you lost yourself in a crowd.
>
> Then, roll +WIS: **on a 10+,** you feel the pull of the grwgnach but shake it off; **on a 7-9**, your mind is briefly adrift among a thousand-thousand others—mark *dazed* but also ask the GM one question about the region, something that at least one starling might know; **on a 6-**, your mind is sucked into the grwgnach—mark *dazed* and you stand rapt until the flock disperses or someone removes you, physically. Also, until you Level Up, your instinct becomes "Birdlike: To act strangely, like a bird."

### **The Halfeyd**

 *Spirit*, *magical*, *fickle*, *sadistic*, *terrifying*

**Instinct** to terrorize its prey

- Manifest as eyes in the dark, watching
- Mark someone as prey
- Set all manner of hunting beasts on their trail
- Put ambush predators in their path

The hunters and trappers of Stonetop know. So do the Hillfolk. The Manmarchers know, and Fen-walkers, too. When you make a kill, you leave an offering. An offering for the Halfeyd. Fail to do so, and you might find yourself as prey—for everything.

**Something interesting:** If the Halfeyd marks you, you'll be hounded for 3 days and 3 nights—even by small predators who would never normally hunt you. **Something useful:** A spirit of running water (like that of a stream) can wash away the Halfeyd's mark.

### **Troelloff**

 *Spirit*, *magical*, *playful*, *cruel*

**Instinct** to confuse, frustrate, delay

- Show warnings left by others: spiral carvings, many-arrowed icons, etc.
- Inflict a brief, intense vertigo
- Make them lose their way
- Show a landmark they've passed before
- Obscure trails, way-signs, and markings

Some places never want you to leave: deep woods, twisting caverns, rugged hills that all look same. Such places give rise to troelloff, maze-spirits, who delight in mischief and feed on the desperation of the lost.

**Something interesting:** Where a troelloff dwells, there's always a true path. It can't harass anyone who stays on this path, but good luck finding it. **Something useful:** The path is never obvious, but it's always marked, by a particular flower, a pattern in the moss, a vein in the stone, etc.
